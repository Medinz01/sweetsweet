ABSTRACT 
The growth of the internet has changed the way people do business and makes it easier for 
software to be delivered as one specific service. An example of this change is the emergence 
of multi-tenant SaaS applications, which allow diverse sellers to create and manage their online 
stores in one ecosystem. This system helps the sellers in that it allows customization of 
storefronts through banners, backgrounds and brand specific themes which aids in reflection 
of their brand identity. Through this ecosystem, sellers are allowed to upload, organize, and 
market any products of their choosing and interact with customers through an easy shopping 
experience. 
The whole design is centred around scalability and security, carefully using modular 
development and database isolation techniques to guarantee strong multi-tenancy. Sellers enjoy 
adequate resource allocation, reduced costs, strong data protection and efficient resource 
utilization. At the same time, the shared platform seamlessly scales with the addition of new 
tenants. The application leverages cutting-edge artificial intelligence, data analytics and 
Integrated Model Context Protocol to deliver sophisticated and powerful solutions for the 
digital commerce ecosystem. 
This project illustrates the efficiency of e-business solutions by not only equipping sellers with 
sophisticated tools to grow their online business but also enabling smart features that improve 
their competitiveness in the digital marketplace. As the internet becomes more prevalent with 
mobile computing, wireless access and new communication technologies, such SaaS platforms 
will be instrumental in the development of the electronic business landscape.

This project centers around the development of a multi-tenant SaaS application enabling

gets the opportunity to personalize their store by adding unique banners, themes, and

1.2 OVERVIEW OF MULTI-TENANT SAAS APPLICATION 
A multi-tenant SaaS application shows a forward-thinking take on software architecture by 
using one platform to serve several different sellers at the same time, while also keeping their 
data and activities separate. Each seller, despite using the same infrastructure, gets the 
impression that they are using a standalone store. This impression is different from single-
tenant setups where separate infrastructure is allocated to every individual seller. The multi-

reduces operational costs, improves scalability and reduces operational costs. Reliability in

integrated using the Model Context Protocol (MCP) and deployed via the programmable 
assistant feature. This kinds of assistant is far from a simple chatbot. By interrogating historical 
sales data, finding behavioral patterns and forecasting demand, this assistant provides sellers 
with predictive insights. Sellers are no longer required to guess which of the products to focus

The rise of cloud computing has changed how we develop applications by altering the 
architecture, distribution, and usage of software applications. Out of the various delivery 
methods of computing services  Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS), Platform as a Service (PaaS), 
and Software as a Service (SaaS) the SaaS model takes the lead by being the most commonly

The idea of multi-tenancy is one of the most important features of this model. Multi-tenancy 
refers to single application instance serving a number of organizations or users (tenants), while 
ensuring their data and the business processes remain functionally segregated. This model 
saves resources, minimizes operational expenditures and provides rapid business updates, 
simplified administration and easy scalability. Thus, sellers and businesses need to concern

2.2 SURVEY ON MULTI-TENANT SAAS AND LEGACY SYSTEMS 
 
The studies done so far have indicated that both academia and industry focus on the efficiency 
and cost savings of multi-tenant SaaS platforms. It is now the focus of research and adoption 
due to the efficiency, scalability and reduced operational overheads. Multi-tenancy systems, 
unlike single-tenant or on-premises systems, which required every organization to maintain its 
own infrastructure, allow multiple tenants to share a single application instance while keeping 
their data logically separated. Scholars believe that the new infrastructure lowers and simplifies 
the cost of delivery, updates, and consistency across tenants. The shifts also enhance the overall 
system manageability. 
 
Most studies being conducted focus on the problems of legacy systems, which still cling to 
monolithic structures and outmoded technologies. These systems still demand a large amount 
of capital and technical resources to operate, yet provide little to no flexibility and system 
integration which the modern enterprise demands. Most upgrades take too long, service 
integrations are convoluted, and sky does the limit on how far the system can be scaled. 
Scholars are in consensus that legacy platforms are unsustainable in fast changing digital 
markets. In contrast, multi-tenant SaaS applications are proven to offer architectural design 
modularises, flexible API-based integrations, and automated scaling, all of which aid in 
servicing wide ranging business requirements and high traffic. 
 
Academics have underscored the role of interoperability protocols for promoting seamless 
relations between SaaS platforms and AI models. The Model Context Protocol (MCP), for 


===== PAGE 19 =====

17 
 
instance, is considered an innovative advancement to enable contextually aware exchanges. AI 
assistants, through the MCP, come to grasp the distinct business landscape of every tenant and 
offer specialized, instead of generic, insights and recommendations. This protocol nullifies the 
distance between the cloud and the intelligent infrastructure, embracing both flexibility and 
value easement. 
 
Studies that compare older systems to SaaS ecosystems have always shown SaaS to be more 
cost-effective, more flexible, more secure, and better performing across the board. SaaS 
systems greatly lower redundancy through shared infrastructure, offer strong data security 
through logical isolation and enable businesses to adopt further functionalities seamlessly and 
within no time. Many data support the claim that the SaaS integration with AI improves the 
underlying competitiveness by offering features such as predictive analytics and intelligent 
decision support which are absent otherwise.

Two kinds of interactions are registered by the server. The first type includes standard HTTP 
requests such as browsing the web, filling out forms, and retrieving information. These requests 
are set up through API routes in Next.js, as is common in web app development. The API 
routes are tightly coupled with the Sequelize datamodels which abstract the underlying 
database. In this design, the interaction with the database is limited to the four basic operations: 
inserts, updates, deletes, and retrieves, involving SQL structured and processed through 
Sequelize. These SQL queries are run on the database which ensures structured and reliable 
data, as well as smooth interaction between the frontend and the backend. 
 
The second stream of interaction focuses on more complex functionalities concerning the 
enabled natural language processing capabilities of the system. Users of the system are no 
longer restricted to making standard requests but can pose questions in their everyday language. 
Fulfilling both host and client roles, the MCP module captures these queries and utilizes its 
smart middleware capabilities to identify natural language queries, commands them, and the 
appropriate calls to the MCP database server. This database server, meant for interfacing MCP 
modules that operate on prepare-and-process request level tool-based communication, 
translates requests into the sufficing executory SQL language of the database. After results are 
retrieved, the MCP module ensures that the responses are unwrapped from the system, 
formatted into English, and sent back to the frontend. 
 
The database is the system's backbone as well as the underlying common knowledge base for both 
streams. It holds all the application data such as users, products, transactions, logs etc. and serves as the 
foundation for both structured API calls and natural language queries. The database can be accessed 
through API route and data models using Sequelize. The MCP module, in contrast, adds freedom and 
logic and enables users to conversate with the system, perform analytic queries and bypass the intricate 
SQL braids  and offers users through certain fully automated insights devoid of the users explicit 
queries.

3. Interaction Flow: The process works as follows: 
 The seller types a natural language question into the chat interface on their admin 
dashboard (e.g., "What are my most popular products?"). 
 The frontend sends this query to a general chat API endpoint (/api/chat/route.js). 
 This endpoint forwards the query to an external Large Language Model (LLM), 
instructing it to use the available MCP tools to answer the question. 
 The LLM analyzes the query and determines that it needs to call the 
get_popular_products tool. It then makes a request to our /api/mcp endpoint with the 
appropriate tool call. 
 Our MCP server executes the get_popular_products function, which runs a SQL 
query to find the best-selling products for that seller. 
 The MCP server returns the structured data (e.g., a JSON array of products) to the LLM. 
 The LLM formats this data into a user-friendly, natural language sentence (e.g., "Your 
most popular products are Product A, which sold 50 units, and Product B, which sold 
45 units.") and sends it back. 
 The final response is displayed to the seller in the chat interface. 
This architecture allows for powerful, natural language-based data exploration without 
exposing the database directly or requiring complex frontend logic.

4.1 TEST INFO 
A combination of manual and automated testing was used to verify the correctness of the platform. The 
testing process was structured around different levels, each focusing on a specific aspect of the system. 
 
Unit Testing: 
Unit tests were focused on verifying the smallest individual components of the application in isolation. 
For the backend, this involved testing individual API route handlers and helper functions to ensure they 
correctly processed inputs and produced the expected outputs. For example, a unit test for a data 
validation function would check that it correctly identifies both valid and invalid data formats. 
 
Integration Testing: 
Integration tests were designed to verify the interaction between different components of the system. A 
key focus was testing the integration between the Next.js API routes and the Sequelize models to ensure 
that data was being correctly passed to and retrieved from the database. For instance, an integration test 
would simulate an API call to create a product and then query the database directly to verify that the 
record was created with the correct attributes. 
 
System Testing (End-to-End): 
System testing involved testing the application as a whole from an end-user's perspective. This was 
primarily accomplished using Cypress, an automated end-to-end testing framework. Cypress tests were 
written to simulate real user workflows, such as a seller registering, adding a product, and a customer 
subsequently purchasing that product. These tests interact with the application through the browser, 
clicking buttons, filling out forms, and asserting that the UI updates as expected. This approach is 
invaluable for catching regressions and ensuring that key user journeys remain functional after new 
code changes are introduced.

5.1 CONCLUSION & FUTURE WORK 
 
The previous study backing the chapter illustrates the importance of multi-tenant SaaS platforms in the 
new digital ecosystem. It has been clear from the introductory part that SaaS has transcended the bounds 
of a low-cost software provisioning model to an innovatively strategic partner of Artificial Intelligence

environments.
